Description of Good Attack

In the case of the good attack, the harmonic envelope increases quite rapidly and linearly until reaching a region more flat. The onset of the note is characterized by a short peak of the envelope of fluctuations, probably due to the tongued attack, before the envelope then sets to lower steady state values. The noise ducking time estimated is very short, finishing after the initial noise peak when the harmonic envelope prevails. The estimate of attack duration toff is found once the harmonic envelope reaches values close to its steady state.

Description of Attack with Delayed Stabilization of Resonance

In the case of attack with delayed stabilization of resonance, the sound starts muffled due to sub-optimal configuration of the oral cavity and air flow of the musician, before reaching a more full sound. This causes the harmonic envelope to rise unstably and with a slower slope than in case of proper attack sounds. This is also reflected in longer estimates of attack time duration and noise ducking time.

Description of Attack with Breath Noise

In the case of the attack with breath noise, the lips of the musician do not start oscillating right away and the sound starts after an undefined period of low energy noise in which air passes through the instrument without generating sound. The adaptive threshold is able to identify the initial breath sound, which contains almost no harmonic content. When the player's lips enter in the regime of self-sustained oscillation, the harmonic envelope starts to rise, although not very stably. Due to this initial breathy part, the estimated attack duration and noise ducking time are much longer than in the case of good attack.

Accuracy and Robustness Analysis

The following plots present the histogram distributions of attack durations estimated using three different methods:

  • Weakest-effort method: A simple approach that estimates attack duration with minimal constraints.
  • -3 dB below the maximum value: Defines the attack duration threshold at -3 dB relative to the peak amplitude.
  • Pitch-synchronous wavelet transform (PSWT) method: A more refined technique that achieves more reliable estimates by separating the harmonic components from the noisy transient.
In order to estimate the statistics, we applied each method to a set of 100 synthesized sounds with attack transients corrupted by enveloped white Gaussian random noise across six levels of signal-to-noise ratio (SNR): 120, 20, 12, 7, 3, and 1 dB. The indicated SNR is defined as the 20 log10 ratio of the maximum levels achieved by the envelopes, which basically is the maximum SNR for at least the first half of the signal duration, including the attack phase. Each histogram displays the distribution of attack durations, along with the average value and standard deviation for each method, highlighting their accuracy and robustness.

Discussion of Attack Duration Estimates

The histograms presented above provide statistical insights into the accuracy and reliability of three different methods for estimating attack duration across various synthesized signals. The results highlight how these methods behave under different noise conditions.

At SNR = 120 dB (virtually no noise), all three methods generally yield a single, well-defined attack duration, indicating their consistency in near-ideal conditions. However, as noise increases (lower SNR values), the estimated attack durations begin to exhibit greater variability, forming a distribution of values instead of a unique estimate.

Among the three methods, the weakest-effort method, as defined in the Timbre Toolbox, is found to be highly unreliable for determining attack duration since it yields much higher standard deviations compared to the other two methods.

In contrast, the -3 dB method and the Pitch-Synchronous Wavelet Transform (PSWT) method both exhibit a Gaussian-shaped distribution around toff, showing greater accuracy. The PSWT-based approach, in particular, demonstrates the lowest standard deviation, making it the most stable and robust method for estimating attack durations in the presence of noise.

Modulation Depth Analysis at Fixed SNR=12

The following histograms present the distributions of attack durations estimated using the three different methods, applied to 100 synthesized sounds with attack transients corrupted by enveloped white Gaussian random noise. In this analysis, the Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) is fixed at 12, while the frequency modulation is set at fm = 4, and the modulation index varies across cmp = [0, 0.0005, 0.0008, 0.001].

The parameter cmp represents the modulation index, expressed as a percentage of the fundamental frequency f0. This index determines the depth of the frequency modulation applied to the signal.

Given a periodic signal with a fundamental frequency f0, represented as:

wave(f0 t)

the modulated signal is constructed as:

wave(f0 (1 + cmp / 100 · sin(2π fm t)) · t)

where fm is the modulation frequency, which is set to 4 Hz in this example. This modulation introduces a periodic variation in the fundamental frequency, simulating a vibrato effect with an increasing depth determined by cmp.

Each histogram displays the distribution of attack durations, along with the average value and standard deviation for each method, highlighting their accuracy and robustness.

Discussion on Modulation Depth Analysis

The histograms above illustrate the statistical distribution of attack duration estimates obtained using the three methods at a fixed Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) of 12 dB, while increasing the depth of frequency modulation of the synthesized signal. This variation simulates a sound with progressively stronger vibrato, adding an additional challenge to the estimation process.

As observed in previous analyses, in view of the high standard deviation attained the weakest-effort method proves to be the least robust of the three methods we explored.

The other two methods - the -3 dB method and the Pitch-Synchronous Wavelet Transform (PSWT) method - yield more consistent results, forming a distribution centered around toff. Notably, the PSWT-based method exhibits a lower standard deviation, reinforcing its reliability in estimating attack duration even under increasingly challenging conditions, such as signals with stronger vibrato.

Expanding the Analysis to Other Musical Instruments

To further assess the applicability of the proposed algorithm, we extended our analysis to sounds from other musical instruments using the Good-sounds dataset. This dataset provides sound samples annotated with "good attack" and "bad attack," offering a valuable basis for evaluating the algorithm's effectiveness across different timbres and attack characteristics.

Analysis of Flute Sounds

The Good-sounds dataset provides flute sounds annotated with "good attack" and "bad attack," without specifying further details. Three different flute sound samples of the same pitch (i.e., G6) were selected as examples to evaluate the algorithm's applicability. The selected sounds include:

  • One annotated with a "good attack."
  • Two annotated with a "bad attack," specifically:
    • One example resembling a "breath attack" type.
    • One example resembling a "noisy attack" type.

Breathy Attack

In the case of the good attack, the envelopes exhibit a behavior similar to that observed in the trumpet sounds. The envelope of the fluctuations remains low, while the envelope of the harmonic components increases very steeply. This leads to short noise ducking time and attack duration estimations.

In contrast, in the case identified with a noisy attack, the envelope of the fluctuations presents higher values, indicating an unstable behavior of the sound. Additionally, the sound also features a short noise due to the air entering the instrument before the actual sound starts, which creates a mix between a noisy attack and a breathy attack. This is followed by a distinct growling sound characteristic in the attack, resulting in higher estimates of both the noise ducking time and the attack duration.

Similarly to the trumpet case, the attack identified with breath noise shows an initial segment characterized by breath noise as air enters the instrument before the actual note starts. In this case as well, the adaptive threshold effectively captures the characteristics of the attack, leading to long estimates of noise ducking time and attack duration.

Analysis of Violin Sounds

The Good-sounds dataset provides violin sounds annotated with "good attack" and "bad attack". Of the sounds classified as bad attack:

  • A group of them does not have any annotation, presenting a noisy transient characteristic.
  • A group of them is annotated with "bad pressure". We interpret this annotation as the player was applying an incorrect pressure of the bow on the string at the attack of a sound. Sounds with this annotation present longer and evident noise in the attack of the sound.
  • A group of them is annotated with "rebond". We interpret this annotation as coming from French, meaning "bounce", so the bow is bouncing on the violin string generating a very short note at the attack of the sound. This is reflected in the behaviour of the sound. This is not a type of sound that we intend to include in our analysis as the type of error is not related to the duration of the attack transient, but to the behavioural characteristics of having one short sound followed by the note.

For the good attack example, the envelope of fluctuations remains at low values even during the attack of the sound, while the harmonic envelope exhibits a smooth increase until it reaches a more steady level. In contrast, the bad attack examples show significantly higher values in the envelope of fluctuations during the attack transient, and the harmonic envelope presents a more unsteady behavior. In these cases, the noise ducking time emerges as the most salient feature to distinguish between good and bad attacks, showing substantial differences in values between the two scenarios.

Depending on the particular type of instrument or articulation, both the estimated attack duration and the noise ducking time proved to be important features for describing the transient attack characteristics of musical sounds.
